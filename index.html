This section will also highlight the structure of RNA, which is typically single-stranded and differs from DNA in several key ways. It will explain the components of RNA, including nucleotides that are made up of a sugar molecule (ribose), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (adenine, uracil, cytosine, or guanine). DNA
This section will highlight the structure of DNA, which consists of two complementary strands arranged in a double helix. It will explain the components of DNA, including nucleotides that are made up of a sugar molecule (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (adenine, thymine, cytosine, or guanine). It will also illustrate the complementary base pairing between nucleotide bases, where adenine (A) pairs with thymine (T) via two hydrogen bonds, and cytosine (C) pairs with guanine (G) via three hydrogen bonds.

Function of DNA and RNA:
This section will explain the fundamental functions of DNA and RNA in cells. It will highlight how DNA serves as the genetic material that carries the hereditary information required for the development, growth, and functioning of all living organisms. It will explain the process of DNA replication, where DNA is duplicated during cell division, ensuring that the genetic information is passed on accurately to the next generation. It will also describe the process of transcription, where RNA is synthesized from DNA, and translation, where RNA is used as a template to synthesize proteins, which are essential for the structure and function of cells

Significance
This section will emphasize the significance of DNA and RNA in various biological processes. It will explain how DNA serves as the basis for inheritance, allowing the transfer of genetic traits from one generation to another. It will also highlight the role of DNA and RNA in genetic disorders and diseases, as mutations or alterations in DNA or RNA can result in genetic abnormalities. It will also highlight the importance of RNA in regulating gene expression, splicing, and other cellular processes, making it a crucial molecule in the regulation of biological functions.
